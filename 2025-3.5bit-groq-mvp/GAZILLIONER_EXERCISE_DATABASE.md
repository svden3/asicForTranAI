### Exercise 1.1: The 60/40 Portfolio (Beginner)

**Scenario**:
Your friend tells you they have a "60/40 portfolio." What does this mean?

**Options**:
A) 60% stocks, 40% bonds
B) 60% bonds, 40% stocks
C) 60% cash, 40% stocks
D) 60% crypto, 40% real estate

**Correct Answer**: A

**Explanation**:
A 60/40 portfolio is a classic balanced allocation: 60% equities (stocks) for growth, 40% fixed income (bonds) for stability. This is considered moderate risk - not too aggressive (100% stocks), not too conservative (100% bonds).

Historical returns: ~8% annually with lower volatility than 100% stocks.

**FQ Impact**: +2 points (Knowledge: Asset Allocation)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"The 60/40 portfolio has been the gold standard for decades. During the 2008 crash, it fell 23% while a 100% stock portfolio fell 37%. Less pain for only slightly lower long-term returns."

### Exercise 1.2: Rebalancing Basics (Beginner)

**Scenario**:
You start with $10,000: $6,000 in stocks, $4,000 in bonds (60/40).

After one year:
- Stocks grew to $7,200 (20% gain)
- Bonds stayed at $4,000 (0% gain)

Your new allocation is now 64% stocks, 36% bonds.

What should you do to maintain your 60/40 target?

**Options**:
A) Do nothing - let winners run
B) Sell $480 of stocks, buy $480 of bonds
C) Sell all stocks and buy bonds
D) Buy more stocks to increase growth

**Correct Answer**: B

**Calculation**:
- Total portfolio: $11,200
- Target 60% stocks: $6,720
- Current stocks: $7,200
- Sell: $7,200 - $6,720 = $480

**Explanation**:
Rebalancing forces you to "sell high, buy low" mechanically. You're selling stocks after they've grown (high) and buying bonds that underperformed (low). This maintains your risk level and captures profits.

**FQ Impact**: +2 points (Discipline: Rebalancing Adherence)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"Rebalancing is counterintuitive - it FEELS like you should let winners run. But research shows rebalancers outperform by 1-2% annually because they systematically sell high and buy low."

### Exercise 1.5: Concentration Risk (Advanced)

**Scenario**:
Your portfolio:
- $50,000 in Apple stock (inherited from parents)
- $20,000 in S&P 500 index fund
- Total: $70,000

What is your Apple concentration, and what is the risk?

**Options**:
A) 40% concentration - too high
B) 70% concentration - extreme risk
C) 40% concentration - acceptable for high conviction
D) 50% concentration - ideal

**Correct Answer**: A (but C is also debatable)

**Explanation**:

- $50k direct Apple

This is EXTREME concentration risk. If Apple crashes 50%, you lose $25,700 (37% of your portfolio).

Rule of thumb: No single stock should exceed 10-15% of your portfolio.

### Exercise 1.7: Dollar-Cost Averaging (Beginner)

**Scenario**:
You have $12,000 to invest. Which strategy is lower risk?

**Options**:
A) Invest $12,000 today (lump sum)
B) Invest $1,000/month for 12 months (dollar-cost averaging)
C) Wait for a market dip, then invest all
D) Invest $6,000 today, $6,000 in 6 months

**Correct Answer**: B

**Explanation**:
Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) reduces timing risk. By investing monthly, you buy:
- More shares when prices are low
- Fewer shares when prices are high

This averages out your cost basis and reduces the risk of investing all your money at a market peak.

Caveat: Statistically, lump sum (A) outperforms DCA 60% of the time because markets trend up. But DCA feels safer psychologically.

**FQ Impact**: +2 points (Discipline: Contribution Streak)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"Morningstar studied lump sum vs DCA over 90 years. Lump sum won 68% of the time. But DCA helps you sleep at night, which matters more than 2% extra return."

### Exercise 1.10: Lifecycle Investing (Expert)

**Scenario**:
A 25-year-old has $50,000 to invest for retirement at 65. Which strategy maximizes expected returns?

**Options**:
A) Invest all $50k in stocks today
B) Use 2x leverage to invest $100k in stocks (borrow $50k)
C) Dollar-cost average $1,250/year for 40 years
D) Wait for a crash, then invest

**Correct Answer**: B (controversial but mathematically correct)

**Explanation**:
Lifecycle investing (Ayres & Nalebuff) argues young investors should use leverage because:
1. You have decades to recover from losses
2. Your future earnings are "bond-like" (stable income)
3. Your portfolio is too small to diversify across TIME

By using 2x leverage at age 25, you simulate having invested your age-50 salary at age 25.

Caveat: This is ONLY appropriate for:
- Young investors (20s-30s)
- Stable income (can cover margin calls)
- Strong stomach (volatility doubles with leverage)

**FQ Impact**: +8 points (Wisdom: Advanced Strategy)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"Lifecycle investing is mathematically optimal but psychologically brutal. A 50% market crash becomes a 100% loss with 2x leverage. Only 1 in 100 investors can handle this."

### Exercise 2.1: Tax-Loss Harvesting Basics (Beginner)

**Scenario**:
You bought Tesla at $300. It's now $200 (down $100/share, -33%). You own 10 shares.

If you sell today, you realize a $1,000 loss. How can you use this loss on your taxes?

**Options**:
A) Deduct $1,000 from your income (save $240-370 in taxes)
B) You can't use losses - only gains are taxable
C) Offset $1,000 of capital gains from other investments
D) Roll the loss forward for 10 years

**Correct Answer**: C (A is also partially correct)

**Explanation**:
Tax-loss harvesting lets you offset capital gains:
1. You sell Tesla for a $1,000 loss
2. You use this loss to offset $1,000 of gains from selling Apple (tax saved: $150-200)
3. If you have no gains, you can deduct $3,000/year from ordinary income
4. Remaining losses roll forward forever

Tax savings: $1,000 loss × 15-20% capital gains rate = $150-200 saved.

**FQ Impact**: +2 points (Knowledge: Tax Optimization)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"Tax-loss harvesting is 'free money.' If you harvest $10k in losses annually and invest the tax savings, you'll have an extra $100k+ over 30 years."

### Exercise 2.2: Wash Sale Rule (Intermediate)

**Scenario**:
On December 15, you sell 100 shares of Apple at a $2,000 loss to harvest the tax benefit.

On December 20 (5 days later), you rebuy 100 shares of Apple because you're bullish.

Can you claim the $2,000 loss on your 2025 taxes?

**Options**:
A) Yes - you sold at a loss
B) No - wash sale rule violation (you rebought within 30 days)
C) Yes, but only $1,000 (50% allowed)
D) Yes, if you buy in a different account (IRA)

**Correct Answer**: B

**Explanation**:
The wash sale rule disallows losses if you rebuy the "same or substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale.

Timeline:
- Dec 15: Sell Apple (loss)
- 30-day window: Dec 15 - Jan 14 (61 days total: 30 before + 30 after + day of sale)
- Dec 20: Rebuy Apple → WASH SALE

Result: The $2,000 loss is DEFERRED (added to your new cost basis), not lost forever. But you can't claim it on 2025 taxes.

Fix: Wait 31 days to rebuy, or buy a similar stock (MSFT, GOOGL) to maintain tech exposure.

**FQ Impact**: +3 points (Knowledge: Tax Optimization)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"The IRS wants to prevent you from selling for tax benefits while keeping the same position. Wait 31 days or buy a similar asset (not identical). Violating wash sale = losing your tax deduction."

### Exercise 2.4: Capital Gains Holding Period (Beginner)

**Scenario**:
You buy stock on January 1, 2025. You sell on December 30, 2025 (held 364 days).

Is this a long-term or short-term capital gain?

**Options**:
A) Long-term (held almost a year)
B) Short-term (must hold >365 days for long-term)
C) Short-term (must hold >366 days for long-term)
D) Depends on the stock

**Correct Answer**: B

**Explanation**:
The IRS requires >365 days (1 year + 1 day) for long-term capital gains.

364 days = SHORT-TERM (taxed as ordinary income, up to 37%)
366 days = LONG-TERM (taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income)

Tax impact on $10,000 gain:
- Short-term: $3,700 tax (37% bracket)
- Long-term: $1,500 tax (15% rate)
- Savings from waiting 2 more days: $2,200

Always check your holding period before selling.

**FQ Impact**: +2 points (Knowledge: Tax Optimization)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"Selling 1 day too early can cost you thousands in taxes. Set calendar reminders for your 366-day anniversary. A little patience = huge tax savings."

### Exercise 2.5: Tax-Gain Harvesting (Advanced)

**Scenario**:
You're a college student earning $15,000/year from a part-time job. You have $50,000 in stocks with $10,000 in unrealized gains.

Your long-term capital gains tax rate is 0% (because your income is below $44,625).

What should you do?

**Options**:
A) Nothing - never sell winners
B) Sell the stocks, realize $10k gain (pay $0 tax), immediately rebuy (step up cost basis)
C) Wait until you're earning more to sell
D) Donate the stocks to charity

**Correct Answer**: B

**Explanation**:
Tax-GAIN harvesting is the OPPOSITE of tax-loss harvesting. When your tax rate is 0%, you should:
1. Sell stocks with gains
2. Pay $0 in taxes (because you're in the 0% LTCG bracket)
3. Immediately rebuy the same stocks
4. Your new cost basis is $10,000 higher

Why? You "stepped up" your cost basis for free. When you sell later (at a higher income), you'll pay less tax.

Example:
- Original cost basis: $40,000
- Current value: $50,000
- Gain: $10,000
- Tax: $0 (0% LTCG bracket)
- New cost basis after rebuy: $50,000

Later (when you're earning $100k):
- Sell at $70,000
- Gain: $70k - $50k = $20,000 (instead of $30k if you hadn't harvested)
- Tax saved: $1,500 (15% × $10k)

**FQ Impact**: +5 points (Wisdom: Advanced Tax Strategy)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"Most people think 'don't sell winners.' But if you're in the 0% LTCG bracket (low income), selling and rebuying is FREE money (step-up cost basis). Do this every year while in school/early career."

### Exercise 2.6: Qualified Dividends (Intermediate)

**Scenario**:
You own two stocks:
- Stock A: Pays $1,000 in QUALIFIED dividends (15% tax)
- Stock B: Pays $1,000 in ORDINARY dividends (37% tax)

How much more tax do you pay on Stock B?

**Options**:
A) $150
B) $220
C) $370
D) No difference

**Correct Answer**: B

**Explanation**:
- Qualified dividends: Taxed at capital gains rates (0%, 15%, 20%)
- Ordinary dividends: Taxed as income (up to 37%)

Tax on Stock A: $1,000 × 15% = $150
Tax on Stock B: $1,000 × 37% = $370
Difference: $220 more tax on Stock B

What makes dividends "qualified"?
- Paid by US corporations (or qualified foreign corps)
- Stock held for >60 days during 121-day period around ex-dividend date

Most blue-chip stocks (Apple, Microsoft) pay qualified dividends. REITs and MLPs pay ordinary dividends.

**FQ Impact**: +3 points (Knowledge: Tax Optimization)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"REITs (real estate investment trusts) pay high dividends (6-8%) but they're ordinary income (high tax). Hold REITs in your IRA to avoid the tax hit. Hold stocks with qualified dividends in taxable accounts."

## CATEGORY 3: OPTIONS STRATEGY (10 Exercises)

### Exercise 3.1: Covered Call Basics (Beginner)

**Scenario**:
You own 100 shares of Microsoft at $300/share ($30,000 position).

You sell 1 covered call:
- Strike: $320
- Expiration: 30 days
- Premium: $5/share ($500 total)

If Microsoft closes at $310 at expiration, what happens?

**Options**:
A) You're assigned, sell shares at $320
B) Call expires worthless, you keep $500 premium
C) You lose money because stock didn't reach $320
D) You must buy back the call

**Correct Answer**: B

**Explanation**:
At expiration:
- Microsoft: $310
- Call strike: $320
- Call status: Out-of-the-money (OTM)

When a call is OTM, it expires worthless. You keep the $500 premium and still own your 100 shares.

Total outcome:
- Share value: $31,000 ($310 × 100)
- Premium kept: $500
- Unrealized gain: $1,000 (shares up from $300 → $310)
- Total profit: $1,500

**FQ Impact**: +2 points (Knowledge: Options Strategy)

**Follow-Up Lesson**:
"Covered calls generate income when stocks trade sideways. You collect $500 monthly on a $30k position (2% monthly yield = 24% annualized). But you cap upside if the stock rips past $320."

### Exercise 3.2: Covered Call Assignment (Intermediate)

**Scenario**:
Same as Exercise 3.1, but Microsoft closes at $330 (above the $320 strike).

What happens?

**Options**:
A) You keep shares and premium
B) You're assigned, sell 100 shares at $320, keep $500 premium
C) You owe the buyer $1,000 (difference between $330 and $320)
D) You can choose whether to sell or keep shares

**Correct Answer**: B

**Explanation**:
At expiration:
- Microsoft: $330
- Call strike: $320
- Call status: In-the-money (ITM)

ITM calls are automatically assigned. You MUST sell your 100 shares at $320.

Total outcome:
- Sold shares: $32,000 ($320 × 100)
- Premium kept: $500
- Original cost: $30,000
- Total profit: $2,500

But you MISS the move from $320 → $330 ($1,000 of upside). This is the trade-off of covered calls: income vs capped upside.
